UNDERSTANDING P/E RATIOS — A COMPLETE GUIDE
What is the Price-to-Earnings Ratio?
The P/E ratio answers one question: how many years of current earnings are you paying for the stock today? A P/E of 20x means the market is paying €20 for every €1 of annual earnings — or equivalently, if earnings stayed flat forever, it would take 20 years to earn back the purchase price.
P/E Ratio = Share Price ÷ Earnings Per Share (EPS)
Example: Stock at €40 · EPS = €2.00 · P/E = 40 ÷ 2 = 20x
TTM P/E (Trailing Twelve Months) uses the last 12 months of actual reported earnings — it's backward-looking and can be distorted by one-off items.
Forward P/E uses analyst consensus estimates for the next 12 months — it's forward-looking and generally more useful for valuation decisions, but relies on forecasts being accurate.
The P/E Spectrum — What Each Range Signals
Negative / 0x5–10x10–20x20–30x30–50x50–100x100x+
NEGATIVE / N/A
— (no P/E)
Company is currently loss-making. No meaningful P/E can be calculated on negative earnings. Common in early-stage companies, deep cyclical downturns, or after large one-off write-downs.
Examples: BioNTech post-COVID, Tesla in early years, most biotech pre-revenue.
How to react
Use EV/Sales or EV/EBITDA instead. Focus on cash burn rate and path to profitability. High risk.
VERY LOW
1–10x
Stock is either deeply undervalued or the market is pricing in a sharp earnings decline. Classic "value trap" territory — cheap for a reason, or a genuine bargain. Common in mature cyclicals (oil, steel, banks) near earnings peaks.
Examples: European banks, steel producers at cycle peaks, commodity companies.
How to react
Investigate why it's cheap before buying. Check if earnings are cyclically elevated. If earnings quality is solid and the business is stable, this can be an exceptional entry point.
FAIR VALUE
10–20x
The historical long-run average for broad stock markets (S&P 500 ~16x, DAX ~14x). Considered fair value for a stable, mature business with modest growth. Offers a reasonable margin of safety without requiring heroic growth assumptions.
Examples: Consumer staples, utilities, traditional industrials, most European blue chips.
How to react
Generally a reasonable entry point. Confirm earnings are stable and the business has a clear moat. Neither a bargain nor expensive — valuation is not the key risk here.
GROWTH PREMIUM
20–35x
Market is paying a premium for above-average earnings growth. Justified if the company can grow earnings at 15–25%+ annually. The PEG ratio (P/E ÷ growth rate) becomes useful here — a PEG below 1.0 suggests the premium is supported by growth.
Examples: SAP, ASML, quality tech incumbents, IFX forward P/E at cycle trough.
How to react
Acceptable if growth is confirmed. Calculate PEG ratio. Verify analyst consensus on forward earnings. Any earnings miss will hurt — build in a margin of safety.
HIGH / SPECULATIVE
35–60x
Valuation embeds aggressive growth expectations. The stock is priced for perfection — any disappointment in earnings, margins, or guidance triggers outsized price falls. Earnings recovery stories (like IFX TTM) can legitimately sit here if a trough is clearly behind the company.
Examples: IFX TTM P/E (54.9x), high-growth software, semiconductor cycle-trough situations.
How to react
Rely on forward P/E, not TTM. Confirm earnings recovery is underway with actual quarterly data. Reduce position size to reflect elevated downside risk. Set a clear exit thesis.
VERY HIGH
60–100x
Market is pricing in multi-year transformational growth. Valuation requires sustained earnings compounding to justify — the margin for error is extremely thin. A single quarter of guidance reduction can cause 20–40% drawdowns. Sentiment-driven as much as fundamental.
Examples: RHM TTM P/E (89.6x), defence super-cycle thesis, high-growth AI plays.
How to react
Only hold if you have high conviction in the multi-year growth story AND the company is executing flawlessly. Monitor earnings quarterly. Consider taking partial profits into strength. Never size too large.
EXTREME
100x+
Earnings are negligible relative to price — the stock is priced almost entirely on future optionality and narrative, not current earnings power. Even a modest earnings shortfall can be catastrophic for the share price. Often seen during bubble periods.
Examples: Nvidia at peak AI enthusiasm, Tesla at COVID highs, meme stocks.
How to react
Treat as a speculative position only. Size must reflect the risk — maximum 2–5% of portfolio. Have a clear stop-loss level. Do not let a winning position become an oversized one.
Five Rules for Using P/E Correctly
1 Never compare across sectors
A tech company at 30x is not necessarily more expensive than a bank at 8x. Different industries have structurally different P/E ranges. Always compare a stock to its own sector median and to its own history.
2 Prefer forward P/E for cyclicals
For semiconductors, defence, and industrials, TTM earnings reflect the recent past — often a trough or a peak that will not repeat. Forward P/E anchored to normalised earnings is a more honest valuation measure.
3 P/E is not a timing signal
A stock can be "expensive" at 40x for years if earnings grow fast enough to grow into the valuation. Conversely, a stock at 8x can fall further if earnings collapse. Valuation sets the risk/reward, not the entry timing.
4 Use PEG to adjust for growth
PEG = P/E ÷ Expected Growth Rate. A PEG of 1.0 means you're paying exactly one year of earnings growth per unit of P/E — roughly fair. PEG < 1 suggests undervalued relative to growth; PEG > 2 suggests very expensive.
5 P/E is one input — never the whole answer
High-quality businesses with strong free cash flow, durable competitive advantages, and pricing power deserve higher P/E multiples than mediocre businesses. A "cheap" P/E on a structurally declining business is often a trap. Always combine P/E with free cash flow yield, return on equity, and balance sheet strength.
